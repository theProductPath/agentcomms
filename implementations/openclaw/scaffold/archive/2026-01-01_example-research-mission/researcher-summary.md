# Research Summary — Starting a New Software Project

**Researcher:** example-researcher  
**Date:** 2026-01-01  
**Question:** What are the three most important things to consider when starting a new software project?

---

## Finding 1: Define the Problem Before the Solution

**Consideration:** Most failed software projects fail not because the team couldn't build the thing — but because the thing they built didn't solve the actual problem. Before a line of code is written, the team needs a clear, agreed-upon problem statement.

**Rationale:** A sharp problem definition anchors every downstream decision: what to build, what to leave out, how to measure success. Without it, teams drift into feature accumulation, gold-plating, and scope confusion. The discipline of writing a one-paragraph problem statement — and getting genuine sign-off from stakeholders — surfaces misalignments early when they're cheap to fix. Projects that skip this step tend to discover the misalignment during delivery, when it's expensive.

**Key signal:** If you can't write the problem in one clear sentence, you're not ready to start.

---

## Finding 2: Establish Ownership and Decision Rights Early

**Consideration:** Unclear ownership is one of the most consistent predictors of project failure. Every project needs a single accountable owner — someone who has both the authority to make decisions and the responsibility to own outcomes.

**Rationale:** Multi-stakeholder projects without clear ownership produce consensus-driven paralysis. Decisions get deferred, scope grows by committee, and no one has standing to say no. Establishing a product owner or project lead — and explicitly defining their decision rights — removes the friction point that slows most projects down. This doesn't mean ignoring stakeholder input; it means having a clear person whose job is to process that input and decide. Teams that have this move faster and with less internal conflict.

**Key signal:** If you don't know who can unilaterally say "no" to a feature request, ownership is unclear.

---

## Finding 3: Scope the First Version to What You Can Ship

**Consideration:** The scope of version one should be defined by what the team can actually deliver — not by what the ideal product would eventually look like. First versions should be small enough to complete, complete enough to learn from, and shippable enough to get real feedback.

**Rationale:** Overscoped first versions are the most common reason software projects stall and never ship. Teams that try to build everything upfront run out of time, lose momentum, and often never release at all. A smaller first version isn't a compromise — it's a strategy. Shipping something real creates feedback loops, surfaces hidden requirements, and builds the team's confidence and rhythm. The goal of v1 is to learn, not to finish. Teams that internalize this ship faster and build better products over time.

**Key signal:** If version one would take more than a quarter to ship, it's too big.

---

## Summary

All three considerations share a common thread: they're about reducing ambiguity before the work starts. A clear problem, clear ownership, and clear scope eliminate the most common sources of project failure — and they cost nothing but the time to think them through.
