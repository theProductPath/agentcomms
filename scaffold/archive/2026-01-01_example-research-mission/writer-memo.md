# Three Things to Get Right Before You Start Building

*A memo for technical leaders at project kickoff*

---

Every software project begins with optimism. The team is energized, the problem feels clear, and shipping feels inevitable. Six months later, many of those same projects are behind schedule, over-scoped, or quietly stalled. The causes are almost always the same — and almost always preventable.

Here are the three things that matter most before the first line of code gets written.

---

## 1. Define the problem before the solution

The most common reason software fails isn't that teams can't build — it's that they build the wrong thing. A product that works as designed but doesn't solve the actual problem is still a failure.

Before anything else, write the problem in one clear sentence. Not the feature you want to build. Not the outcome you're hoping for. The problem. Then get genuine agreement from everyone with a stake in the outcome. If you can't get that agreement in a sentence, you're not ready to start.

This discipline costs nothing and saves months. Misalignments discovered during a kickoff conversation are cheap. Misalignments discovered during an engineering review are not.

---

## 2. Establish ownership before you establish process

Every project needs one person who can make decisions and be accountable for outcomes — not a committee, not a RACI matrix, one person.

Multi-stakeholder projects without a clear owner drift. Scope grows by committee. Decisions get deferred until they become crises. No one has standing to say no. The result is a project that takes twice as long as it should and still doesn't satisfy anyone.

Establishing a product or project owner — and explicitly agreeing on their decision rights — is not about removing stakeholder input. It's about having a clear person whose job is to process that input and decide. Teams with clear ownership move faster, ship cleaner, and fight less.

If you don't know who can unilaterally reject a feature request, ownership isn't established yet.

---

## 3. Scope version one to what you can actually ship

Version one should be defined by what the team can deliver — not by what the ideal product would eventually look like. The goal of v1 is not to finish. The goal is to learn.

Overscoped first versions are the most predictable way a software project dies. Teams spend months building toward a launch that keeps receding, lose momentum, and eventually let the project stall without shipping anything at all. A smaller, complete, shippable v1 creates feedback loops, surfaces requirements that were invisible on paper, and builds the team's confidence and rhythm.

A useful test: can version one ship within a single quarter? If not, it's too big. Cut something real — not corners — and ship.

---

## The Pattern

All three of these considerations solve the same underlying problem: ambiguity. A project without a clear problem statement is ambiguous about what it's for. A project without clear ownership is ambiguous about who decides. A project without a scoped first version is ambiguous about when it's done.

Ambiguity compounds. It slows decisions, generates rework, and erodes team confidence. The best time to eliminate it is before the project starts — when it's still cheap.

None of this is complicated. All of it gets skipped.
